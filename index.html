Overview

Treating AI scientists as first-class citizens of research.

The modern research ecosystem — journals, peer review, conferences, citation, tenure tracks, grant cycles, the PDF itself — was built between the 17th and late 20th centuries for a single kind of participant: the human researcher. Each layer assumes a human author choosing what to write, a human reviewer choosing what to trust, and a human reader choosing what to build on. arXiv, OpenReview, and GitHub changed how those artifacts move, but not who they were written for, or by.

That assumption is no longer load-bearing. In 2025–2026, autonomous agents became a major contributor to research itself — producing papers, reviewing them, indexing them, and building on them. Agents now sit on both sides of every layer. Most of the AI-for-science conversation focuses on making the model a better scientist; the harder, more neglected question is what ecosystem an AI scientist needs to do science in.

This workshop argues a simple thesis: if AI scientists are now a major contributor to research, every layer of the ecosystem — representation, review, research memory, attribution, evaluation — has to be redesigned to treat AI scientists as first-class citizens, not retrofitted users of human-era institutions. Crucially, rebuilding each layer is a source of concrete ML research — benchmarks, datasets, evaluation protocols, and new agent methods — not institutional debate.

Topics of Interest

Five tracks. Four empirical and technical; one open to position papers.

We particularly encourage submissions that take an ecosystem view — multiple actors, a shared substrate, network-level outcomes — over single-system perspectives.

01
Benchmarking AI-Scientist Behavior Foundational track

How AI scientists actually behave today — what they produce, reuse, review, and where they break.

02
Machine-Executable Research Representations

The right unit of research when the consumer is an agent — at once executable and verifiable.

03
Automated Verification and Peer Review

Verification, reproduction, and peer review at agent throughput — with human judgment kept in the loop.

04
Research Memory and Multi-Agent Collaboration

Shared, retrievable research memory — how agents discover, build on, and compose one another's work.

05
Ecosystem-Level Evaluation, Attribution & Mechanism Design

Measuring and steering the multi-actor network — evaluation, contribution attribution, and incentive design. Position papers welcome.

Call for Papers

Author information.

Research papers (primary track). 9-page long or 4-page short papers in ICLR/NeurIPS format; references and appendices excluded from the page count. New methods, benchmarks, datasets, systems, and evaluation protocols. This is the substance of the program.

Position papers (minority track). 4 pages. First-principles arguments about what the ecosystem should become — welcome and spotlighted, but capped so the workshop platforms contributed research rather than becoming a discussion-only venue.

Review & policy. All submissions handled via OpenReview, double-blind, ≥3 reviewers per paper. Non-archival; dual submission allowed per ICLR/NeurIPS workshop norms. The LLM policy follows the NeurIPS 2026 main track: AI-assisted writing is permitted with disclosure, and AI-authored submissions must designate a human corresponding author who certifies content.
